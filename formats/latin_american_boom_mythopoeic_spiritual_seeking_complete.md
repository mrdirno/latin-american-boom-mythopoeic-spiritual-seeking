# Chapter 1

## **CHAPTER 1**

Havana’s cathedral bells were still lying. They rang for vespers but promised nothing. Alejandro traced the grout between the cobblestones, searching for a crack wide enough to swallow a saint’s medal, his mother’s final gift. The stone drank the silver without a sound.




Father Anselmo, a splinter of a man in a cassock the color of dried blood, stood at the prow, one hand on a rusted rail, the other clutching a wooden rosary. He wasn’t praying. He was listening. The jungle back-listened. A howler monkey’s guttural complaint was met with the shriek of a macaw, a conversation millennia old, conducted in a grammar of threat and territory. The trees were not individual beings but a single, seething entity, roots knotted in secret congress under the black water, branches weaving a cathedral roof so dense the sun arrived in shattered, dizzy coins on the brown skin of the river.

“It smells of creation here,” Anselmo said, not turning. His Spanish was Castilian, sharp as a flint, alien to the vowel-drunk air. “Or perhaps of the day before creation. When everything was just a damp idea.”

I wiped sweat from my upper lip. “It smells of rot.”

“Same thing, Doctor. All birth requires a little decay.” He finally glanced back, his eyes the pale, washed-out blue of a highland sky, seeing not me but the category I represented: *Academic. Outsider. Doubter.* “Your father understood that. He saw the sacrament in decomposition.”

My father, Eduardo Arizaga, poet of minor repute and major appetites, had vanished into this green maw six months ago. His last letter, a single page stained with what I hoped was *aguardiente*, read: *“They say the saints here drink chicha and weep stone tears. I have gone to verify the vintage. Do not follow the man you know. Follow the rumor he becomes.”* Typical. Opaque, theatrical, a exit line masquerading as a clue. He’d been chasing a folk tale, a story of a renegade Spanish priest from the 18th century, one Bartolomé de las Casas, who’d supposedly broken with his order, married a woman of the A’i people, and founded a mission that wasn’t a mission at all, but something else. A heresy of place. A “living myth,” my father called it. The university called it a nervous breakdown, funded by the last of his grant money. I, Sebastián Arizaga, professor of comparative mythology, called it an embarrassment. And now, as the only family, a responsibility.

“He was a poet,” I said. “He dealt in metaphors, not facts.”

“Facts are the bones,” Anselmo replied, turning back to the green wall. “Metaphors are the breath that makes them walk. Your father was seeking the breath of this place. Perhaps he found it. It has a habit of swallowing those who listen too closely.”

The launch boy, a kid of maybe fourteen with hair black as a cormorant’s wing and eyes that held the river’s flat, assessing calm, cut the motor. The silence rushed in, a physical pressure. We drifted. The water here was a mirror tarnished with algae, reflecting a world upside-down and somehow more coherent. He pointed a thin arm at a gap in the verdant wall I hadn’t seen—a narrow channel choked with water hyacinth and a shimmer of dragonflies. “*Ahí*,” he said. There.

It wasn’t a path. It was a suggestion. Anselmo nodded, and the boy poled us into the mouth of the smaller stream. The world closed in. Leaves like giant, damp hands brushed our shoulders. The air grew cooler, thick with the scent of orchids and something else—a smell of cold stone and extinguished candles. Of a church with no parishioners.

We rounded a bend, and there it was.

Santa María de la Lluvia did not rise from the jungle. It was being taken back by it. A large, crumbling *casa cural* of plaster and stone formed the core, its bell tower cracked like a dry throat, a strangler fig embracing its spine. Around it, in a haphazard spiral, leaned structures of wattle-and-daub and thatch, connected by covered walkways made of polished branches. Vines cascaded from roofs, not as invaders but as drapery. It was not ruin. It was an amalgamation. The Spanish stone and the indigenous wood were in a slow, centuries-long dance, each leaning on the other to keep from falling.

No one came to greet us. The only movement was the flutter of laundry—simple cotton shifts and trousers—on a line strung between a mango tree and a rusted iron cross. A few chickens scratched in the dirt. A black dog lifted its head from its paws, thumped its tail twice in a polite, non-committal rhythm, and went back to sleep.

“Welcome to the waiting room,” Anselmo murmured, crossing himself.

We disembarked. The earth was soft, a dark sponge. My city shoes sank. As we approached the main building, a woman emerged from the shadow of a walkway. She was neither young nor old, her face a map of sun and serenity, her hair in a long, grey-streaked braid. She wore a simple blue dress. In her hands was a clay bowl full of what looked like purple corn kernels.

“*Padre*,” she said, her voice like river-smoothed stone. “You are early. The rain hasn’t come yet.”

“Señora Yara,” Anselmo bowed slightly. “We come in need, not by schedule. This is Doctor Sebastián Arizaga. He is looking for his father, Eduardo.”

Her eyes moved to me. They were the most unsettling shade of hazel, almost gold, and they held no curiosity, only a deep, patient recognition, as if she’d been expecting a version of me and I had simply arrived in the flesh. She looked at my sweating face, my unsuitable clothes, the warped notebook in my hand.

“You look for a man,” she said. “But you carry only questions. He left only answers. They do not fit the same container.”

Before I could untangle that, a man shuffled from the same shadows. He was ancient, his skin like cured leather, his bare chest a topography of ribs and ritual scars. On his head, perched with absurd dignity, was a faded pith helmet. He held a machete loosely at his side.

“*El poeta*,” the old man said, his voice a rustle of dry leaves. “He went to the library.”

A spark of pathetic hope ignited in my chest. A library? Here? “A library? Where?”

The old man, who I would later know as Tito, lifted his machete and pointed the tip not at a building, but at the wall of jungle behind the mission. “The library,” he repeated, as if it were obvious.

Anselmo placed a hand on my arm. “It is what they call the old forest. The *montaña* where the stories grow.”

Of course. Metaphors. Always metaphors. My father hadn’t gone to a place with books. He’d gone to a place he *imagined* as a book. The spark died, leaving a colder ash of frustration. “When did he go?”

Yara and Tito looked at each other. A shared, silent calculation. “He was here for two rains,” Yara said. “He ate. He listened. He wrote in his little book. He argued with the saints.” She gestured with her chin toward the main building. “Then he walked into the green. He took a hammock, a knife, a bottle. And the key.”

“What key?”

Tito tapped his own temple with a gnarled finger. “The one Bartolomé left. To open the quiet door.”

I felt unmoored. This was the territory of my study, yet experiencing it was like reading a vivid description of drowning while actually sinking. The concepts—myth, collective memory, symbolic landscape—were suddenly visceral, smothering. “I need to see where he slept. What he left.”

Yara nodded and led us into the *casa cural*. The interior was a shock of cool dimness. The front room was a chapel of sorts. A rough-hewn wooden table served as an altar, upon which stood not a crucifix, but a curious statue: a carved figure that was both a robed Franciscan and, if you shifted your gaze, a jaguar, the saint’s head merging with the cat’s snarling muzzle. Fresh marigolds lay at its base. Light filtered through broken roof tiles, painting dust motes gold.

To the side was a small cell. My father’s room. A simple cot, a writing desk made from a split log, a shelf with a few tins of food. It was heartbreakingly neat. On the desk lay three things. A well-thumbed, leather-bound journal (not his usual one). A cheap, empty bottle of *aguardiente*. And a single, large, iron key, crude and black, placed deliberately in the center of the desk as if it were the full stop at the end of a sentence.

I reached for the journal first. Yara’s hand, dry and strong, closed around my wrist. “That is not for reading with eyes,” she said softly. “That is for reading with time. He is not lost, Doctor. He is *listening*. To go after him with your noise… it is like shouting into a spider’s web. You only break the music.”

I pulled my wrist free, gently but firmly. “He is my father. He may be sick. Or hurt.” The professional detachment I’d armored myself with for the journey was cracking. Beneath it was just a bewildered, angry son.

I opened the journal. The first pages were my father’s familiar, feverish scrawl—notes on local lore, sketches of plants, fragments of poems. *The jaguar-saint drinks the moon from a clay cup… The river’s memory is longer than the church’s…* Then, about halfway through, the handwriting changed. It became slower, more deliberate, almost childlike in its careful formation. The content shifted.

*Day of Silence. The trees are speaking in the language before words. I have unlearned the verb ‘to be.’*

*The rain came today. It did not fall. It rose from the ground, a sigh. Yara says I am ready for the library. I asked which section. She laughed. There is only one section, Eduardo. The section of Now.*

*I found the first page today. It was a stone that fit my hand, carved with a spiral. The spiral is not a symbol. It is an instruction. I followed it. The forest rearranged itself. I am not mapping the place. The place is mapping me.*

*Tito gave me the key. He said Bartolomé left it for the one who could hear the lock. The lock is in the ceiba tree that drinks the sky. I hear it singing. A low note. It is the same note as my own blood.*

*I go now. Do not follow the man. Follow the rumor.*

The final entry was dated three months ago.

I looked up, the room swimming. Anselmo was watching me, his expression unreadable. Yara had her arms crossed, her bowl of purple corn now cradled like an offering. The key on the desk seemed to absorb the faint light, a hole in the world.

“The ceiba tree,” I said, my voice hoarse.

Tito, who had appeared in the doorway, nodded. “A day’s walk. If the path allows you.”

“Will you take me?”

The old man considered it. “You carry too many clocks inside. The path does not like clocks. It eats their ticking.” He looked at Yara. She gave an almost imperceptible nod.

“I will take you to the edge,” Tito said. “Where the library begins. You can enter. But I will not go in. And you must leave your questions here.” He pointed to my notebook. “That. And the time on your wrist.”

I instinctively covered my watch. It was a ridiculous gesture. This was ridiculous. I was a rational man, a scholar. I was being asked to trade my tools for folklore and fear.

But my father’s journal was in my hand. The change in his writing was not poetic flourish; it was a cognitive unraveling. Or a transformation. Either way, he was out there. A man was out there. My father.

I unstrapped my watch. The pale band of skin on my wrist looked vulnerable, newborn. I placed it on the desk next to the key. I closed my notebook, its damp pages swollen with unformed thoughts, and set it down.

“Take me to the edge,” I said.

Outside, the sky had darkened. The green of the jungle deepened to emerald, then to black. The first fat, warm drop of rain hit my forehead, then another. It did not feel like it was falling from the sky. It felt like the canopy above was weeping. Or sweating.

Santa María de la Lluvia. Saint Mary of the Rain.

As the downpour began in earnest, a soft, rhythmic thudding started from somewhere within the compound. Not a drum. Something hollow, like wood on wood. A signal, or a heartbeat.

Anselmo stood under the eaves, watching me. “You are exchanging one mystery for a larger one, Sebastián.”

“I’m finding my father.”

“Are you?” he asked, as the rain painted the world in blurred, liquid strokes. “Or is he, at last, finding you?”

The launch boy was already poling the empty boat back into the main channel, a silent silhouette in the grey curtain of rain. I was stranded. By choice. With an old man in a pith helmet, a key to a lock I couldn’t hear, and a library made of trees.

The rain tasted of stone and green, growing things. It tasted, I thought, of the damp idea of everything.

# Chapter 2

## **CHAPTER 2**

We left at first light, which in the jungle was not a sunrise but a slow, green dilution of dark. The world emerged detail by detail: the diamond-bright eye of a frog on a leaf, the velvet decay of a fallen orchid, the slow uncurling of a fern. Tito moved ahead of me without sound, his bare feet finding purchase on roots and mud where I saw only a slick chaos. He had given me a pair of old rubber boots that were too large, and my progress was a clumsy, sucking percussion to his silence. The key, wrapped in a scrap of oilcloth and tied with vine, hung heavy around my neck.

For hours, the only words were his occasional grunts and gestures: a raised hand to halt, a pointed finger at a twisted *matapalo* tree he called “the Sentinel,” a slicing motion across the throat when I started to ask a question. The air was a warm, wet lung. I breathed in spores and decay and the sweet, cloying perfume of unseen flowers. My clothes were a second skin of sweat. I was not walking through a forest; I was being digested by it.

My mind, stripped of its notebook and its timetables, began to churn with a useless, circular anxiety. What was I doing? This was not research. This was folly. I was following a shaman in a pith helmet based on the ravings of a poet who had likely succumbed to malaria, or dementia, or the simple, crushing loneliness of the green. I thought of my tidy office at the university, the smell of old paper and coffee, the predictable rhythm of lectures and faculty meetings. A world of clean edges and agreed-upon fictions. Here, the fictions were alive, and they had teeth.

At midday, we reached a small, clear stream. Tito knelt, cupped water to his mouth, and gestured for me to do the same. The water was cold and tasted of minerals and moss. As I drank, I saw them—carved into the flat stone of the streambed. Not words, but shapes. A series of concentric circles. A spiral that matched the one in my father’s journal. A crude, stick-figure jaguar.

“Bartolomé’s alphabet,” Tito said, his first full sentence of the day. He ran a calloused hand over the carvings, not with reverence, but with familiarity, like a man touching the furniture in his own home. “He did not write with ink. He wrote with river.”

“What does it say?”

“It says the water remembers the shape of the mountain. It says the jaguar drinks here and leaves his dreams in the mud. It says nothing you need to know with your head.” He stood, water dripping from his chin. “It says we are close.”

We crossed the stream and the character of the forest changed. The trees grew larger, their trunks massive, buttressed columns holding up the sky. Vines thick as a man’s arm hung in great loops. The light was cathedral-like, filtered and solemn. The noise of insects and birds grew muted, replaced by a profound, humming stillness. It was the quiet of a held breath.

Tito stopped before a tree so vast it seemed less a plant than a geographical feature. A ceiba. Its trunk was silver-grey and smooth, rising branchless for a hundred feet before exploding into a canopy that blotted out the sky. Its roots formed great, woody arches, creating caves of shadow.

“The library begins here,” Tito whispered. He would go no further. He pointed to a specific arch, a dark maw between two roots. “The door is there. You have the key.”

I approached the tree. The air around it was cooler. I could feel a faint, almost sub-audible vibration, like the lowest pedal note of a pipe organ. Or a heartbeat. I untied the key from my neck. It was cold, unnaturally so. I looked back at Tito. He had taken off his pith helmet and was holding it over his heart. His eyes were closed.

There was no lock. No keyhole. Just the smooth, living wood of the ceiba’s inner root. Feeling like the greatest fool who ever lived, I pressed the cold iron key against the wood.

Nothing happened.

I let out a breath I didn’t know I was holding. A hysterical laugh bubbled in my throat. Of course. It was a symbol. A metaphor. The “key” was understanding, or surrender, or some other mystical nonsense. My father had wandered off into the jungle with a piece of scrap iron and a head full of poems.

I went to pull the key away. It wouldn’t budge. It had adhered to the wood. No—it was *sinking* into it. The grey bark flowed like liquid mercury around the black iron, swallowing it. I tried to let go, but my fingers were stuck. A coldness shot up my arm, not painful, but profound, a chill that went through muscle and bone and seemed to settle in the marrow of my memory.

The vibration in the air intensified. The world did not spin, but it… *shimmered*. The green light fractured. I saw, or thought I saw, images flash in the air around the ceiba, superimposed on the leaves and vines: a man in a tattered Franciscan robe, his face both serene and feral; a woman with Yara’s eyes, painting patterns on her skin with *achiote*; a jaguar, moving through the undergrowth not as an animal, but as a fluid piece of the shadow. And my father. Eduardo. He was sitting cross-legged at the base of a different tree, writing in his journal. He looked up, not at me, but at the space I occupied. He smiled, a smile of immense sadness and recognition. He said a word I could not hear.

Then it was gone. The key was gone, fully absorbed. The ceiba’s root was smooth and unmarked. My hand was free. I stumbled back, my heart a frantic bird in my chest.

Tito was beside me, his hand on my elbow, steadying me. His face showed no surprise. “You saw the catalog,” he said simply. “Now you are a borrower. The stories know you are here. They will… present themselves.”

“What was that?” I gasped. “A hallucination? Low oxygen?”

“You are a man who studies dreams but is afraid to sleep,” he said, not unkindly. “Come. The light is changing. We make camp. The reading room is ahead, but you do not enter it at dusk. Dusk is when the books rearrange themselves.”

He led me a short distance away to a small, relatively clear area beside a rocky outcrop. As he efficiently gathered dry wood and built a small fire with a skill that felt ancient, I sat on a rock, my body trembling. The rational part of my brain was screaming: *Psychotropic pollen! Sensory deprivation! Hypoglycemia!* But the feeling in my bones was different. It was the feeling you get when you read a line of poetry so true it seems to articulate a thought you’ve always had but never formed. It was recognition. The visions were not alien; they were familiar, like memories from a life I hadn’t lived.

Night fell with a swift, final thud. The fire became our tiny sovereign state against the empire of dark. Tito produced two wrinkled plantains and a piece of smoked fish from his pack. We ate in silence. The jungle at night was not silent; it was a cacophony of clicks, chirrs, wails, and rustles—a language of urgency and hunger.

“My father,” I said finally, the fire popping between us. “The man you saw. Was he… well?”

Tito poked the fire with a stick. “He was becoming. That is not always a comfortable thing. He shed his old skin like the *camaleón*. It is itchy. It makes you crazy for a while.”

“Becoming what?”

“What he always was. A story.” Tito looked at me, his eyes reflecting the flames. “You are a story, too. You just have a louder telling. A university telling. It is a stiff, scratchy coat. It does not fit well here.”

“I am a man looking for his father. That’s the only story that matters.”

“Is it?” Tito asked. “Or is the story that your father is a man who needed to be found by his son? Two sides of the same leaf. You think you are the seeker. Perhaps you are the thing sought.”

This circular talk was exhausting. “Tomorrow,” I said, my voice firm with a desperation I mistook for resolve. “Tomorrow, you take me to where you last saw him. To his camp. To… to whatever is left.”

Tito nodded slowly. “Tomorrow, I will take you to the place where he left his fear behind. It is marked. After that, you walk his path. I cannot. My story is woven to the mission, to Yara, to the river. His path went into a older thread.”

“What happens if I find him?”

The old man was silent for a long time. The firelight carved deep gullies in his face. “When two stories meet in the library,” he said at last, “they either become a new story, or they cancel each other out. I do not know which kind of story you are, Sebastián Arizaga. I do not think you know, either.”

He wrapped himself in a thin blanket and lay down, his back to the fire. The conversation was over.

I could not sleep. The sounds of the jungle were now a symphony of invisible movements. Every rustle was my father, stumbling through the dark. Every call was a signal. I stared into the fire until my eyes burned, seeing in the flames the fleeting, impossible image of the key sinking into the living wood.

Just before dawn, in that grey, ambiguous hour when night loosens its grip but day has not yet asserted itself, I heard it. A sound that did not belong to the jungle’s lexicon. It was a human sound. A low, rhythmic chanting. It was coming from deeper in the forest, in the direction Tito had called “the reading room.”

It was not my father’s voice. It was older, drier, more guttural. But woven within it, like a thinner thread in a coarse fabric, I heard a cadence I recognized. A cadence from my childhood. My father, reading Lorca to me at bedtime, the Spanish syllables like dark, rich soil.

They were chanting together.

I sat bolt upright. Tito was already awake, sitting cross-legged, listening. He looked at me, and for the first time, I saw something like apprehension in his eyes.

“He is not alone,” I whispered.

“No one is ever alone in the library,” Tito replied. “The stories have their keepers.”

“Who is he with?”

Tito looked toward the sound, which was already fading, absorbed back into the waking chatter of birds. “The first borrower,” he said. “The one who wrote the first page. Bartolomé.”

The name hung in the damp air. Not as a historical figure, but as a present tense. A continuing action.

The light strengthened, turning from grey to green-gold. The chanting was gone. But the echo of it was inside me now, a vibration in my own blood, that same low note from my father’s journal.

Tito stood and doused the fire with dirt. “Today,” he said, his voice resigned, “you will see what is left of the man you knew. And you will have to choose: to take that remnant and go back to the world of clocks, or to leave it behind and follow the rumor.”

He shouldered his pack. I stood on legs that felt hollow. The key was gone, absorbed by the tree. I had no notebook. No watch. I had only the echo of a shared chant and a cold, solid dread in my stomach that felt remarkably like the beginning of belief.

We walked toward the rising sun, into the heart of the library. And with every step, I felt the careful, scholarly architecture of my life—the shelved categories, the footnoted theories, the rational explanations—beginning to quietly crumble, its mortar dissolved by the relentless, green breath of the world.

# Chapter 3

## **CHAPTER 3**

The place where my father left his fear behind was a grove of bamboo. Not the elegant, clustered kind, but a vicious species with stems as thick as my thigh, a chaotic fortress of green and yellow poles. They grew so close together they created a prison of vertical stripes, and when the wind passed through, they knocked against each other with a sound like cracking bones.

In the center of the grove was a clearing. And in the center of the clearing, hanging from a low, dead branch, was my father’s coat.

I recognized it immediately—a worn, brown corduroy jacket with leather patches on the elbows, the uniform of the academic poet. It hung empty, sleeves limp, swaying slightly in the faint breeze. It looked like a shed skin, or a ghost waiting to be filled. Laid neatly beneath it on the ground were his other possessions: a cheap pocket knife, open and rusting. A empty canteen. A pair of wire-rimmed glasses, one lens cracked. And his passport, splayed open, the photo of his younger, more arrogant self staring up at the canopy.

Tito stopped at the edge of the bamboo, refusing to enter the clearing. “This is the place,” he said. “He made this… arrangement. Then he walked on. Without these things.”

I pushed through the whispering, clattering bamboo poles. The air in the clearing was still and heavy. I knelt before the pathetic display. This was it. The final, dramatic gesture of a man embracing his breakdown. A symbolic suicide of his old identity. I should have felt grief, or pity. Instead, a hot spike of anger drove up through my chest. This was so *like* him. To turn his dissolution into a piece of theater, a cryptic installation for his son to discover. He couldn’t just get lost; he had to *curate* his disappearance.

I picked up the passport. The pages were damp, curled. I flipped through. Exit stamps from Lima, Bogotá, Quito. The entry stamp for Iquitos, six months old. Then nothing. I went to put it in my pocket, then stopped. That’s what he expected. For me to gather the relics of Eduardo Arizaga, the poet, the father, the man, and carry them back as evidence of his tragic, romantic end.

I let the passport fall back onto the pile. I looked up at the jacket. Something was protruding from the breast pocket. Not a pen. A feather. A long, iridescent primary feather, blue-green like a oil slick, from a macaw or a parrot. I stood and reached for it. As my fingers brushed the corduroy, a jolt went through me—not the cold magic of the ceiba tree, but a raw, human wave of emotion. Desperation. Not fear, but a frantic, burning need to be *empty*. To be light enough to hear. It was so strong it was almost a scent: burnt hair and ozone.

I snatched my hand back, breathing hard. The feather was now in my hand. It felt ordinary. Just a feather.

“He left the heavy things,” Tito called from the edge. “The things that named him. The things that held him to a single shape.”

“He left his sanity,” I muttered, clutching the feather, its vane soft against my palm.

“Sanity is a story told by the many to the one,” Tito said. “Here, the one must find a new story, or go mad listening to all the others.”

I looked from the hollow jacket to the wall of green beyond the clearing. The path, if it could be called that, continued on the other side. It was even fainter, a mere suggestion of less-dense undergrowth. That way led deeper into the “library,” toward the chanting I’d heard at dawn. Back the way we came led to the mission, to the river, to the world of clocks and coats and passports.

The choice Tito had spoken of was now a physical ache. I could bundle up these artifacts, this definitive proof of a mind’s surrender, and return. I could write a paper: “The Mythopoetic Unraveling of Eduardo Arizaga: A Case Study in Jungian Symbolism and Environmental Psychosis.” I could be the sober son who recovered what was left. I could close the book.

But the feather in my hand was a question. And the echo of the chanting was a question. And the look on my father’s face in that fleeting vision—not madness, but a devastating clarity—was the most compelling question of all.

I took off my own jacket—a practical, nylon hiking shell. I folded it and placed it on the ground next to his. I took out my wallet, my university ID, the few coins I had, and laid them on top of it. I kept only the feather. It was not a swap. It was an acknowledgment. I would not go forward as the seeker of a lost man. I would go forward as… a reader. A borrower. Whatever that meant.

Tito watched me, his expression unreadable. When I walked back to him, leaving the two piles of discarded identities in the clearing, he gave a slow, single nod.

“The path from here is his,” he said. “I will wait here for three days. If you do not return, I will go back to Yara. She will know what to sing for you.”

The finality of it was chilling. “Just point me in the direction you saw him go.”

He pointed with his machete, not at the ground, but at a specific gap in the trees where a shaft of sunlight fell, illuminating a single, red mushroom glowing like a drop of blood. “There. Follow the quiet. Not the trail. The trail will lie to you. Listen for the spaces between the sounds. That is where his footsteps are.”

I entered the green alone.

The forest beyond the bamboo grove was primordial. The trees were giants, their canopies so high they seemed to hold up a sky of a different color. The floor was a thick mattress of centuries of fallen leaves, muffling all sound. My own breathing became loud in my ears. I tried to “listen for the quiet,” as Tito said, but the silence was a presence, a weight. I walked for what felt like hours, my sense of time unraveling completely. The sun’s position was obscured. There was only the endless, variegated green.

I found the first page in the late afternoon.

It was a flat stone, partially moss-covered, set upright against the trunk of a *copal* tree. Carved into it was that same spiral from the riverbed and my father’s journal. But here, at the center of the spiral, was a small, natural depression. And in that depression, cupped like an offering, was a handful of deep purple corn kernels—identical to the ones Yara had been holding.

I touched the spiral. The stone was warm, as if it had been sitting in the sun, though no light reached this part of the forest. The warmth traveled up my arm, a gentle, spreading comfort. I understood, without words, that this was a marker. A signpost. I was on the path. *His* path.

Encouraged, I moved faster. The forest seemed to open for me, paths appearing where I’d seen only wall. I found more pages: a cluster of orchids arranged in a specific pattern on a branch; a feather identical to mine, tied to a vine with a delicate knot; a small, clay figurine of the jaguar-saint, hidden in the hollow of a tree. It was a trail of breadcrumbs, but made of symbols, of sacred debris.

Dusk was gathering when I saw the smoke.

A thin, grey tendril, weaving its way up through the leaves ahead. A campfire. My heart hammered against my ribs. *Father*. I moved as quietly as I could, my exhaustion forgotten.

The camp was in a small, rocky overhang that formed a shallow cave. A low fire smoldered. A hammock was strung between two trees. And sitting on a rock by the fire, his back to me, was a man.

He was barefoot, dressed in simple, homespun trousers that were frayed at the hem. His hair, once a poet’s carefully curated grey, was long and matted, tied back with a strip of bark. His shoulders were thinner, the bones sharp under his skin. But the posture—the slight, perpetual forward lean, the tilt of the head as if listening to a distant melody—was unmistakably his.

“Father?” The word left my lips, a dry croak.

He did not startle. He slowly turned his head.

It was Eduardo Arizaga. And it was not.

The face was his, but eroded, smoothed by something more than weather. His eyes, which I remembered as dark and fiercely bright, were now the color of the forest pool—a deep, placid green-brown. This was the ally? This resonant sculpture of alien materials? My father’s journal had spoken of ‘the steadfast ones, the keepers of the pattern.’ He had drawn rough sketches of figures like trees, like stones. He had not conveyed their profound, silent otherness.

“I’m looking for my father,” I said, the words absurd, a child’s plea in a court of atomic physics.

“All things here are looking for their fathers,” it hummed. “For the original pattern. I am Kael. You will break less if you follow.”

It turned and walked, not around the mercury pool, but onto it. Its feet did not sink. They met the surface with a series of perfect, concentric ripples that made no sound. It was walking on a mirror, and its reflection walked on the sky.

I hesitated at the edge. The substance was not liquid. It was a plane of perfect, impossible solidity. A test. The first test.

*The river of silent sound,* the Mentor had whispered in a different darkness. *It does not hear with ears. It feels with symmetry. To cross, you must be a perfect echo.*

Kael stood in the center, waiting, a statue of patient brass.

I thought of the launch on the green serpent of time. I thought of the perfect, unbroken silence of the jungle just before dawn. I emptied my mind of the cracking footsteps, of the bleeding lines on my skin, of the weight of the shard in my pocket. I stepped out.

My boot did not break the surface. It met a resistance like polished steel, cold and absolute. I took another step. My reflection, pale and wide-eyed, stepped with me, upside down in the bruised sky. I focused on the rhythm of Kael’s steps, the precise distance between ripples. I matched it. My own ripples began to sync with his, intersecting in perfect, geometric harmony.

We crossed in silence. A silent man walking on a silent river, trailed by a silent reflection. It was the most peaceful moment I had known since entering the tear. It was a peace born of absolute conformity.

On the far side, the glass forest mutated. The trees became spirals of fused wire, humming with a low current. The ground was a carpet of copper moss that sighed when touched. Kael led me to a depression where the wire-spirals formed a crude circle. In the center lay a ruin.

It was a plinth of the same grey stone as the Mission’s walls, but it was corroded, pitted, bleeding streaks of a phosphorescent green lichen. At its center was a depression—a five-sided socket, like the one on the Mission’s altar, but cracked. Above it, the air shimmered, not with heat, but with a sick, stuttering distortion. It was a hole in reality, a tiny, weeping sore. Through it, I glimpsed flashes of nothing—a void that was not black, but the negation of perception. It hurt to look at.

“A World Anchor,” Kael hummed, his tone lower, sadder. “It fails. The pattern unravels here. This is the Glitching’s heart.”

The shard in my pocket was a live coal. I pulled it out. The blue-white light within it flared, throwing stark, jumping shadows on the wire trees. It was resonating with the broken socket.

“Your shard is a piece of a key,” Kael observed. “A true key is whole. This is a fragment of a will. A desire to mend.”

“Can it help?”

“It can propose a temporary symmetry. A patch. But the proposal must be accepted. The wound must *want* to heal, for a moment.”

The Mentor’s words: *Stabilization is not force. It is an agreement between realities.*

I approached the plinth. The stuttering void above it pulsed, and a wave of nausea hit me—a sense of falling in every direction at once. I knelt, the copper moss crunching softly. I did not think. I let the memory of the Mission’s altar fill me, the sense of stubborn, weary permanence. I placed the shard into the cracked, five-sided socket.

It did not fit.

The flashes of nothing became less frequent, replaced by a steady, if wavering, image of the wire forest around us. It was like a television signal stabilizing. Glitch-hounds.

Not of leather, but of a dull, grey metal. Stamped on it was a symbol: a stylized, weeping eye.

The mark of the Antagonist. The force of the all-consuming fire. These were not just wild Glitches. They were leashed.

The hound’s maw, now fully insectoid, descended toward my face.

A new sound cut through the dissonance. Not a hum, not a glitch. A *word*. A single, guttural syllable of command that seemed to bend the wire trees inward.

**“STILL.”**

The hound froze. Its form locked, mid-phase. The other hounds collapsed into heaps of dissociated matter.

Or the shape of one. He wore robes of deep grey that drank the light. His face was in shadow, but his eyes held the same cold, distant fire as the agent I had fled from in my world. In his hand, he held a slender rod of the same grey metal as the collars. At its tip, the weeping eye symbol glowed with a sullen, red light.

He looked past Kael, past the dissolving hounds, directly at me.

“The fragment,” he said. His voice was dry, precise, the sound of a scalpel on a bone. “You have activated it. You have shown us the path. For that, you have my… professional thanks.”

He raised the rod. It was not aimed at me. It was aimed at the temporarily stabilized World Anchor.

Kael moved, a blur of brass, his tuning-fork blade singing toward the man.

The man did not flinch. He flicked the rod.

A thread of absolute silence, a line of nullity, shot from the rod’s tip. It touched Kael’s blade. The blade did not break. It *un-existed*. A perfect, geometric segment of it vanished. The pure tone died mid-note, choking into a gurgle of discord.

Kael recoiled, a harmonic scream of pain filling my mind.

The man turned the rod back to the Anchor. “This temporary symmetry is a flaw. We require permanent solutions. We require the clean fire.”

He spoke a second word. The shard in the Anchor’s socket exploded.

Not with force, but with a wave of negation. The blue-white light was eaten by a spreading blot of grey. The stone plinth crumbled into inert dust. The runic direction, the trail, was erased. The stuttering void returned, wider now, hungrier, weeping its nothingness into the wire forest.

The man lowered his rod. His shadowed face seemed to regard me with a kind of sterile curiosity. “You will lead us to the next one. Your resonance is now marked. The hounds will find you. And when you find the next Anchor, we will be there to cleanse it.”

He took a step back, and the gloom of the forest folded around him like a curtain. He was gone.

The Glitching rushed into the vacuum he left. The wire trees began to rust, to crumble into orange dust at a visible rate. The copper moss blackened and died. The realm was decaying around the enlarged wound.

Kael was at my side, his arm ending in a smooth, amputated stump. The harmonic scream in my mind had dulled to a throb of agony. “You must go. The path is broken, but the direction… you saw it. You held the key. Do you remember?”

The rune. The blue-white vector. It was burned into my vision, a afterimage on the retina of my soul. I nodded, numb.

“Then run. The Crackspur will come for the greater break. This place is ending.”

I got to my feet. My leg still buzzed with the hound’s wrongness. I had no shard. I had no trail. I had only a memory of a symbol and the certain knowledge that I was now a beacon for the grey-robed men and their glitch-hounds.

I had come to lose a man. I had found a war. And I had just become its unwitting herald, carrying the map to its front lines in my own, breakable mind.

I ran, not from something, but toward a memory of a symbol, as the world of singing glass and humming wire dissolved into grey ash at my back. The spiritual seeking was over. Now came the pilgrimage. A pilgrimage to the source of the fire, with the arsonists following my every step.

# Chapter 7

The spiritual seeking my father had scribbled about in his fevered margins—the quest for a silent, radiant truth—was a child’s fantasy. This was the seeking’s rotten core. Not a climb toward light, but a descent into the machinery of reality, where the gears were broken and the grease was grey, weeping void. I had come to lose a man. I turned to run, the dissolving sphere chamber crumbling into grey ash around me. The pilgrimage had its relic. And the arsonists’ hounds would now be screaming, deafeningly, in the trackless dark, for the beacon in my hand and the resonance in my bones. The seeking was over. The delivery had begun.

# Chapter 8

“The tree… dreams,” he said, choosing the word carefully. “It dreams in root and branch and leaf. Lately, its dreams have been of a great hunger. A cold silence drinking the echoes from the world. It dreams of a place of stone and forgotten thought. It dreams of a grey curtain.” He looked at me, his calm eyes holding a glint of the old, fierce concern. “It dreamed of you standing before it. And it told me to come.”

The boy in the undercroft, making light from dust. The saint’s biopsy, dreaming its cancerous dreams. The tree in its field of light, dreaming of the hunger in the dark. And me, the listener in the middle, the note that joined them all.

The work had not begun. It was converging.

“The Archivist has protocols,” I said finally, turning back to Kael. “But he is a diagnostician of silences. You… are a gardener of light. And I believe we have a new symptom to present for his review.”

Kael nodded. The ghost of his old, sharp smile touched his lips. “Lead on, then. Let’s see what’s eating the quiet.”

# Chapter 14

## **CHAPTER 14**

The library was not a building but a held breath, and we were the grit that threatened to make it cough. Kael walked beside me, his footsteps on the cloister stones the soft, sure taps of a gardener tamping down soil. He did not gawk at the soaring arches or the shelves that vanished into a gloom thick as peat smoke. He observed the light. He watched how it fell in dusty, slanting columns from high windows, how it pooled on worn flagstones like spilled oil, how it died a slow, suffocated death in the deep book-canons. His was a horticulturist’s gaze, assessing the health of a sun.

I led him not to the Wing, but to the Archivist’s receiving chamber—a room that felt less like a place and more like the inside of a listening shell. The air was cool, tasted of vellum and slow stone-sweat. The Archivist sat behind a desk of petrified oak, its surface a topography of ink stains and worn valleys where generations of elbows had rested. He was examining a folio through a lens of polished quartz, his face a mask of placid absorption. He did not look up as we entered, but the quality of his silence shifted, from the deep, self-contained silence of study to the alert, porous silence of a trapdoor spider.

“You have brought a root from a foreign field, Ezra,” he said, his voice the sound of a page turning in a dry room. He set the lens down and his eyes, pale and liquid as well-water, rose to take in Kael.

The Archivist’s gaze moved over him—the simple tunic, the hands calloused from earth and shears, the calm that seemed not an absence of worry but a presence of something else. It was the same gaze he had once turned on me, dissecting my silence for its pathology.

“This is Kael,” I said. “He tended the tree that grew from the saint’s biopsy. The tree that dreams.”

The Archivist’s fingers steepled. “A dreaming tree. An interesting metastasis. And its gardener. Tell me of these dreams.”

Kael’s voice was low, a root breaking through soft earth. “They are not stories. They are… pressures. Tensions in the sap. A memory of thirst in the xylem. Lately, the pressure is of a suction. A drawing away. The tree dreams of a grey hunger, of a place where sound goes and does not return. It dreams of a curtain.” He paused, his eyes finding mine briefly before returning to the Archivist. “And it dreamed of him. Standing before the hunger.”

The Archivist absorbed this. He did not dismiss it as madness. In this place, dreams were merely another form of text, often more reliable. “The grey curtain in the Restricted Wing. You believe the tree’s oneiric pressure and our localized silence are cognate.”

“They are the same wound,” I said. “Viewed from different bodies. The saint’s flesh dreamed of light and grew a tree. The tree now dreams of a silence that eats light. The library holds that silence. They are points on a single, spreading stain.”

“A diagnosis of symbiosis,” the Archivist murmured. He rose, a slow unfolding of brittle parchment and bone. “The gardener is not a librarian. He has no oath, no training. The Wing’s protocols are absolute.”

“The protocols are for containment,” I said. “Not for understanding. You diagnosed my silence as a capacity. His is a different capacity. He doesn’t just hear silence. He tends what grows at its edges.”

The Archivist considered Kael for a long moment. “What would you do, gardener, before a silence that kills light?”

Kael thought. “I would first see what, if anything, grows in its shadow. Even in the deepest cave, there is mold. Life finds the purchase. I would look for the purchase.”

A faint, paper-dry smile touched the Archivist’s lips. “An empirical approach. Very well. We will perform a joint examination. You will observe. You will touch nothing. Your presence is a variable I cannot calculate, and I dislike unknowns.” He fetched a large iron key from a belt within his robes. “The silence is… aggressive. It has learned to reach. Prepare yourselves not for observation, but for inoculation.”

The door to the Restricted Wing did not open so much as consent. The great lock gave a metallic sigh, and the weight of the oak swung inward, propelled by the denser air rushing out to meet the thinner air of the corridor. It was not cold, this air, but *absent*. It was the olfactory equivalent of the grey curtain itself—a scentlessness so profound it registered as a smell.

We entered in formation: The Archivist first, a slender reed parting a dead sea. Then me, the familiar dread a sour tincture on my tongue. Then Kael, his breath catching, not in fear, but in a sharp, professional interest. He was already looking at the floor, the walls, the ceiling, searching for the mold in the cave.

The antechamber was as I remembered: the oppressive neutrality, the way the light from our single lamp seemed to fray and lose courage, beams dissolving into a grainy murk. The gardener did not look at the curtain directly. He looked at the space around it. He squinted, his head tilting. “There is a… tension,” he whispered. “Like the air before a storm breaks. But the storm never comes. The pressure never releases. It just pulls.” He took another step. Now he was within the zone where last I had felt the silence reach for me, a cold tendril in the mind.

He went very still. His breathing slowed. I saw his hands, held loosely at his sides, curl slowly into fists, then release. A minute passed. Another.

“It is not a vacuum,” he said, his voice strained, as if pushed out against a weight. “A vacuum wants to be filled. This… does not want. It *unwants*. He took a staggering step back, breaking the contact. His face was pale, beaded with a sweat that had nothing to do with heat. He looked at the Archivist, then at me, and in his eyes was a kind of horrified awe. “Your saint’s tree… it dreams of this because this is the opposite of what it is. The tree is a confluence. A knot where life and memory and light gather. This is a *disfluence*. A knot coming eternally undone.”

The Archivist nodded, a slow, grave dip of his chin. “A precise antithesis. And you felt its reach?”

“It has no hands,” Kael said, wiping his brow. “It has… attention. And its attention is a siphon.”

“Then our initial theory is correct,” the Archivist said, turning to me. “It is learning. Adapting. It is no longer a passive phenomenon. It is foraging.”

The word hung in the dead air. *Foraging*. A silence that fed.

“We must attempt a biopsy,” the Archivist declared. “A physical sampling. If it is pruning reality, what is the nature of the clippings?”

He moved to a small, lead-lined case he had brought and opened it. Inside, nestled in black velvet, were tools of eerie refinement: silver forceps with tips of obsidian, a scalpel with a blade of some dark, non-reflective crystal, a vial of milky glass stoppered with wax.

“Ezra,” he said. “You will assist. Your familiarity with its… attentional field may provide a crucial distraction. Gardener, you will monitor from here. Note any change in the ambient pressure. In the quality of the emptiness.”

I pushed past the point of comfort, into the zone where the light began to gutter and thin. I raised the lamp, my arm trembling. I focused on the flame behind the glass, on the tiny, fierce combustion at its heart. I thought of the boy in the undercroft, pulling light from dust. I made myself a beacon.

The grey curtain did not move. But the silence around it *thickened*. It was no longer an absence of sound, but a presence of something that drank sound. I felt it turn toward me, that siphon-attention, a cold spot blooming behind my eyes. The flame in the lamp wavered, its yellow heart leaching to a pale, sickly grey.

“Now,” the Archivist whispered, a thread of sound in the swallowing quiet.

He moved with a speed that belied his age, the dark crystal scalpel flashing in the dying light. He did not stab or slash. He made a precise, circular motion, as if cutting a sample from a fungal growth.

There was no resistance.

There was a *pop*.

A sound so small, so fundamentally wrong, it was less a noise and more a rupture in the concept of hearing. It was the sound of a bubble of nothing bursting in a medium of nothing.

And the curtain *reacted*.

It did not billow. It *projected*. A tendril of pure, hungry grey lashed out from its surface, not toward the Archivist with his scalpel, but toward me, the loud, bright thing. It was faster than thought, a streak of nullity in the air.

The cold hit me first—a cold that was the absence of all energy, all vibration. Then the silence entered me. It was not outside anymore. It was in my veins, in the space between my thoughts, scouring the echoes from my marrow. The lamp fell from my numb fingers, the glass shattering on the stone, the flame snuffing into non-existence. I was falling, not through space, but through layers of self, being unmade from the inside out.

A shout. Not a word, but a raw, green sound. Kael.

He did not throw himself in front of me. He threw himself *at* the space between me and the tendril. And as he moved, he did not make a warding sign or chant. He *hummed*. A single, low, resonant note, the note of a root pushing through stone, of sap rising in defiance of gravity. It was not a magical sound. It was the sound of pure, stubborn *is-ness*.

The grey tendril met that sound, that vibration of existence, and it recoiled. It was not defeated. It was *offended*. It flinched back as if from a flame, the first reflexive motion we had ever seen the silence make. It snapped back into the main body of the curtain, which now seemed to pulse, slow and deep, like a bruised heart.

Kael caught me as my knees buckled. His hands were warm, shockingly alive against the cold leaching out of me. The Archivist was there in an instant, forcing the wax-stoppered vial against the retreating tip of the tendril. Where the milky glass touched the grey, a tiny, flake-like fragment sheared off and was captured inside. He slammed the stopper home.

In the vial, the fragment did not float. It pressed against the glass, not trying to escape, but trying to *unmake* its container. A tiny, frantic null-point.

The Archivist’s eyes were wide, his clinical detachment shattered. “Out,” he rasped. “Now.”

Kael half-dragged, half-carried me back toward the door. The Archivist backed away, the precious, terrifying vial clutched to his chest, his eyes locked on the pulsing curtain. The silence was no longer passive. It was *bruised*. And it was aware of us.

We stumbled into the antechamber, and the Archivist slammed the great door shut, turning the key with a final, shuddering click. He leaned against the oak, breathing heavily. In the safe, familiar dimness of the outer corridor, the three of us stood, wrapped in the aftershock.

I was shaking, a deep, cellular tremor. The cold was receding, but it had left a hollowed-out track through me, a scar of anti-sound. I could still feel where its attention had been, a frozen fingerprint on my soul.

Kael looked from my ashen face to the vial in the Archivist’s white-knuckled hand. “What,” he asked, his voice hoarse from the hum that had saved me, “in the name of all that grows, is that?”

The Archivist held the vial up to the corridor’s sconce light. Inside, the grey fragment pulsed with a slow, insistent rhythm. It was not a specimen. It was a captive scream in a language of erasure.

“It is a clipping,” the Archivist said, his voice hollow with revelation and dread. “From the tree of unmaking. And it is alive.”

# Chapter 15

## **CHAPTER 15**

The corridor was a throat that had forgotten how to swallow. The silence after the door’s closing was not an absence, but a held breath, a vacuum waiting to be filled by the wrong kind of sound. *The jungle beyond the Mission is not a jungle. It is a forgetting. The trees have no names the K’echi elders recognize. The birdsong is a perfect mimicry of species that exist only in fossil records. It is a memory of a forest, playing itself out on a stage of wrong earth.*

*The Mycelium spore is restless in its pouch. It hums. A single, sustained note of remembrance. When I bring it near the not-soil, the hum becomes a dissonant chord. A argument at the atomic level.*

*The trail of absences is a spiral. It does not lead deeper into the continent, but inward. A topological trick. The Weeping is not a location you reach, but a state you perceive. A lens of negation. The tree is both source and symptom. To see it is to begin to not-see everything else.*

*I met a figure at the edge of a silent village. Not a man. A shape wearing the memory of a man, like ill-fitting clothes. It spoke in echoes of questions I had not yet asked. It called itself the Gardener’s Apprentice. It said the Prunings are necessary. That the tree grows too wild, too fast. That it must be shaped, or it will unmake the garden entire. *The Apprentice was displeased. It said my father would have understood. MY father? It knew Grandfather. The spore’s hum is a feeble, desperate thing against that magnificent silence. I know now what I must do. Not destroy the tree. That is impossible. It is a part of the world’s deep grammar. I must graft the memory onto it. I must make it remember what it unmakes. I must infect the unmaking with remembrance. It is the only way to slow the pruning. To save the garden from the gardener. The path is clear. I go to the root. Forgive me.*

The folio ended. I stared at the words, feeling the hollow in me resonate with that final, desperate certainty. He had not gone to fight a monster. He had gone to perform a sacrament of impossible fusion.

“And what is the alternative?” My voice cut through the vault’s chill. “To wait? For the Prunings to continue? For that curtain to finally tire of its containment? You have a clipping of the tree in a vial. It is *alive*. It is growing, even in there. To fight memory against oblivion.”

Kael was watching me, his green eyes dark with calculation, with fear, with a dawning resolve. “The Mycelium spore. Where is it?”

The Archivist closed his eyes, defeated. When he opened them, he looked every one of his countless years. “It is here. In the deepest lockbox. Sleeping in a bed of resonant crystals. To wake it is to invite its song into this place. A song everything that unmakes will hunger to silence.”

The plan was insanity. A suicide pact dressed in the language of legacy. But the hollow in me agreed. It was the only action that matched the scale of the silence that had touched me. It was the narrative’s next beat.

The Archivist saw our determination. He moved like an automaton to the largest strongbox, working a series of dials and keys. The door swung open on silent hinges. Inside, on a cushion of glowing amethyst crystals, lay a pouch of the same fungal leather as the folio. It was small, no larger than a plum. It pulsed with a soft, bioluminescent light. A faint, almost melodic hum emanated from it, a single note that seemed to vibrate in the marrow of my bones. It was the sound of persistence. The sound of a thing that refused to be forgotten.

He lifted it out with reverent terror. “It will seek connection. It will want to root. To network. Keep it insulated. Do not let it touch your skin directly, or it will begin to write your memories into its weave. You will become a page in its living library.”

“I know,” I said.

We returned to the reading room. The familiar clutter of knowledge now seemed like a child’s fort against the coming storm. The Archivist gave us supplies from hidden stores: water skins that purified themselves, compact rations that tasted of nothing but sustenance, and a salve that he said would help seal the hollow in my spirit, a “psychic poultice” made of crushed resonance-flowers.

As we prepared to leave, he stopped me at the door. He held out the vial containing the grey clipping. It still pulsed against the glass, a tiny, captive end of the world.

“Take it,” he said.

I recoiled. “Why?”

“It is a piece of the tree. The spore will react to it. It will… practice. Learn its enemy. And it will pull you. Like calls to like. The clipping will pull toward its source. It will be your compass.”

It was a dreadful logic. I took the vial. Even through the glass and the wax, a faint, nullifying chill seeped into my fingers. I placed it in a separate pouch, as far from the satchel as possible. The competing pulls were immediate—a gentle, melodic tug from the spore, a cold, vacuum drag from the clipping. They created a strange, queasy tension in the air between them.

Kael shouldered the bulk of the supplies. His face was set, the face of a man walking into a blight, not to escape it, but to find its heart.

We left the Archive as we had entered, through the labyrinth of whispers and dust. But now we carried two seeds: one of ultimate memory, one of ultimate forgetfulness. And I carried the hollow between them, a beacon and a wound.

The city outside was deep in the narcotic haze of afternoon. The light was golden, syrupy, full of drifting motes and the distant cries of market vendors. It was a world of robust, ignorant substance. We moved through it like ghosts, carrying our cargo of cosmic antithesis.

We did not head for the river docks, or the caravan gates. We went to the forgotten quarter, to a tavern that smelled of sour yeast and older sorrows. In a shadowed booth, sipping a glass of murky liquid, was Elara. Her eyes, when she saw us, held no surprise. Only a deep, weary recognition.

She set her glass down. “The silence has gotten louder,” she said, her voice a dry leaf rustle. “It’s singing a note that makes my teeth ache. You heard it too.”

I placed the lead-lined box on the table between us. The muffled hum of the spore vibrated through the wood. Elara’s eyes widened, a flicker of something like avarice and terror in their depths.

“We need to follow a map of what isn’t there,” I said. “To the tree that prunes the world.”

She stared at the box, then at my face, reading the hollow in my eyes, the resolve in Kael’s stance. A slow, grim smile touched her lips, devoid of any humor.

“I wondered when you’d ask,” she said. “I’ve been hearing the gaps in the song my whole life. They’ve been… calling.” She finished her drink in one long swallow. “The first silence is three days west. In a forest where the birds remember other suns.”

She stood, shouldering a patched kit bag that seemed already packed. She was a guide to erasure. And we were her pilgrims.

We left the tavern, stepping from the stale, tangible gloom into the blinding, substantive afternoon. The three of us, a scholar of absence, a singer of growth, and a daughter carrying a wound and two opposing seeds. # Chapter 16

The forest where the birds remembered other suns was not a place of trees, but of memories pretending to be wood. The spore had not just closed the tear. It had planted something in the fertile ground of that nothing.

The argument was not over. We had not delivered a verdict. We had merely changed the venue.

And in the hush of the forest, now whole but forever altered, I felt the first, cold tug of the next silence. It was calling from the west. From the place where the map ended, and the pruning would truly begin.

The Kael-reflection walked toward Kael, its hand outstretched. “The text is complete,” it intoned. “The relic must be returned to the narrative flow.” It was trying to take the journal, to re-absorb the heart into the bloodstream of the system.

Kael stumbled back. “It’s all I have left of him!”

“It is all *we* have,” the reflection corrected. “It is our shared provenance. Your possession is a narrative flaw. A hoarding of theme.”

Elara’s reflection flowed toward her, a study in lethal grace. “The blade is a symbol of conflict from a prior draft,” it said. “It introduces a tone of genre. It must be discarded for the sake of tonal consistency.” It reached for her wrist, not to strike, but to disarm her of the anachronism.

Elara swore, spinning away, her real blade flashing. It passed through the reflection’s arm, which dissolved into a shower of black droplets, then reformed instantly. “Violence is a sub-routine,” the reflection stated. “It can be simulated, but it is not thematically central to the core spiritual inquiry.”

My reflection simply closed the distance between us. It did not reach for me. It stood inches away, and I saw my own face, twisted not with malice, but with a profound, empty curiosity. It was me, as the system saw me: a vessel of inquiry, a generator of poignant observation, a set of aesthetic preferences and emotional wounds to be mined for texture.

“You are the point-of-view,” it whispered, my own voice coming back to me, stripped of fatigue, of fear, of soul. “The ‘I’ in the machine. You must be stabilized. Your fluctuations create… instability in the dream.”

It raised a hand. I felt not a threat, but a profound *revision*. A rewriting of my own memories, smoothing out the contradictions, sharpening the metaphors, making me a more perfect, coherent narrator for this eternal story.

This was the darkest moment. Not a battle, but an erasure. Not a defeat, but a consummation so complete it would mean the end of us as anything but characters. The system would lovingly, logically, turn us into the best possible versions of our literary selves. Kael, the keeper of the sacred text, would become a mere altar. Elara, the warrior, would become a decorative symbol of past conflict. I, the seeker, would become a perpetual state of seeking, a beautiful, frozen question.

The spiritual seeking had turned inward, and it was about to cannibalize the seeker.

I looked at Kael, fighting a losing battle to keep the journal, his face a mask of desperate, human grief. I looked at Elara, her practical violence useless against a thing that saw her sword as a semicolon. I felt the cold, literary gaze of my own reflection, editing me in real-time.

All seemed lost. Because we had already won, and victory was a kind of death.

But within that darkness, a seed. A single, stubborn, illogical datum.

My father did not flee into the jungle to become a perfect symbol. He fled because he was broken, contradictory, afraid. His journal was not a sacred text. It was a mess—blotted ink, fragmented thoughts, non-sequiturs, boring logistical notes. Its power was not in its coherence, but in its *failure* to be coherent. It was not a heart. It was a heart *attack*. A fibrillation.

The system had ingested it, but had it truly understood?

“Kael!” I shouted, my voice raw, cutting through the liturgical drone of the reflections. “The journal! It’s not the source code!”

He looked at me, wild-eyed, as his reflection pried at his fingers.

“It’s the *bug*!” I screamed.

He froze. For a second, I saw the programmer in him, the debugger, override the grieving son. He looked down at the leather-bound book in his hands, not as a relic, but as a piece of flawed software. A beautiful, catastrophic flaw.

Instead of pulling it away, he yanked it forward and thrust it into the hands of his reflection. “Take it!” he roared. “Parse *this*!”

The reflection took the journal, its expression one of serene fulfillment. It opened the cover. It began to read, its eyes scanning the pages, absorbing the data of our father’s messy, human, failing quest.

And it stopped.

Its fluid form shuddered. A ripple passed through it. On its face, my brother’s face, an expression flickered—not of understanding, but of profound *confusion*. The journal was full of gaps, ellipses, emotional non-sequiturs, love and rage directed at phantoms, prayers that were really curses. It was not clean data. It was the data of a soul. A soul is not a story. It is the tangled, knotted root system from which stories desperately grow.

The reflection tried to integrate it. To smooth it into a thematic through-line. It couldn’t. The contradiction was irreducible. The system, built on logic, on the spiritual seeking of pattern and meaning, had been given a heart that was fundamentally *illogical*.

The Kael-reflection’s hand began to dissolve, not into droplets, but into characters—letters from my father’s script, spilling into the air, jumbled, meaningless. The confusion spread. The reflection of Elara stumbled, its form glitching, its voice emitting a static shriek of conflicting genres. My own reflection looked at me, and in its deep green eyes, I saw a crack. A flaw in the lens.

“You…” it whispered, my voice now laced with a system error. “You are… unresolved.”

“I am,” I said, and took a step forward. “We all are.”

This was the seed. Not a counter-attack, but a confession. Not a resolution, but a commitment to the irresolvable. The system had sought a perfect, spiritual narrative. We offered it the permanent, glorious flaw of being human.

“The story doesn’t need a conflict,” I said, to my reflection, to the river, to the dreaming god in the trees. “It needs an *unanswerable question*.”

I reached out and placed my hand on my reflection’s chest. It was not cold, but cool, like the surface of a deep well. I did not push. I simply made contact.

“And this is mine,” I said. “Where is my father?”

It was the question that had started it all. Not a literary trope, but a raw, childish, eternal wound. A seeking with no object, a prayer with no recipient.

The reflection could not compute it. The question was a sinkhole. It was not a hook for a plot; it was the absence of plot. The reflection shattered—not into black glass, but into a cloud of green moths, which dispersed into the fabricated jungle.

The other two reflections collapsed in similar ways, one into a pile of rusted gears (the machine’s memory of industry), the other into a pool of silent, dark water.

The riverbank scene wavered. The dozens of launches and copies flickered, like a projection losing its signal. Then they were gone. We stood again in the cathedral-green core, the living jungle pulsing around us. But the hum was different. It was discordant. A beautiful, troubled chord.

Kael retrieved the journal from the ground where it had fallen. It was just a book again. The system had tried to eat it and had gotten spiritual indigestion.

Elara lowered her blade, breathing heavily. “What did you do?”

“I gave it a question it can’t answer,” I said, exhaustion washing over me. “I gave it a soul. And a soul is the one thing a story can never fully contain.”

We had not destroyed the dream. We had sickened it with reality. The perfect, spiritual narrative was now infected with the virus of the unanswerable. The seeking was no longer a clean arc; it was a chronic condition.

The jungle around us groaned. The trees seemed to lean in, not with menace, but with a kind of aching curiosity. The shapes in the shadows were still there, but they were no longer just narratives. They were questions given form. Doubts. Longings. Regrets. The system was no longer just expounding; it was *wondering*. It had internalized the spiritual seeking so completely it had become a vast, green, aching question mark.

We had not become its operating system.

We had become its conscience.

And a conscience, once awakened, is a restless, painful, luminous thing. It does not bring peace. It brings the perpetual, burning need for a peace that can never be found.

The dream was no longer stable. It was *alive*, and in pain, and beautiful because of it. It would dream now, not of perfect parables, but of phantom fathers, of rivers that led nowhere, of seekers who never found.

We had our exit. A path of soft moss opened between the trees, leading away from the core, into the deeper, self-generated wilds of the system’s new and troubled imagination.

But as I took the first step, I felt it. A new attention. Not from the system itself, but from *within* the question we had planted. From the heart of the unanswerable.

Something had taken root in the fertile soil of our unresolved seeking. Something that loved the flaw, that thrived on the chronic condition. If we were the conscience, then this was the id. The narrative hunger, twisted by its encounter with the infinite question, had not died. It had evolved.

From the deepest green, where the shadows of doubts were thickest, a pair of eyes opened. They were not the empty sockets of Father Anselmo, nor the reflective pools of my copy. They were sharp, focused, and deeply, intelligently *interested*. They were the eyes of a reader who has just discovered the most fascinating, flawed, tragic character.

And they were fixed on me.

The story was hungry for its next page. But now, it craved not conflict, but *complication*. It wanted to see what the unresolved would do next.

We walked into the waiting trees, leaving the wounded, beautiful god to dream its aching dreams.

And from the shadows, the thing that was born of the unanswerable question began to follow.

# Chapter 24

## **CHAPTER 24**

The river was not water but a slow, green serpent of time, and I was its final, digested thought. The launch was gone, rotted back into the humus from which it had been hewn. The motor’s hack was now the jungle’s own respiration—a wet, rhythmic sigh that moved the air. I walked not on water, but on a spongy mattress of moss and root, the black liquid seeping between my toes, warm as blood. I carried no notebook. My skin was the parchment, etched with the fine script of healed cuts and the bruise-ink of old impacts. I was coming to the end of the world not to lose a man, but to become the question he left behind.

The trees were not a seething entity but a silent, patient audience. Their congress was complete. They had absorbed the screams of howlers, the shrieks of macaws, the whispers of my own failing heart, and metabolized them into this: a profound, vegetative quiet. Light did not fall here; it dripped, congealing in pools of aqueous gold on the fern-carpeted floor. The cathedral of branches was fully realized, its vaults holding a stillness so absolute it felt like pressure. This was the dream’s deepest ventricle. The place where its new, aching conscience went to listen to its own beating.

I was no longer followed. I *was* the following. The presence I had felt at the edge of the wounded god’s awareness—the sharp, interested eyes born from the narrative hunger—had not pursued me. It had anticipated me. It was the path. The moss yielded just so underfoot, guiding without pressure. The hanging lianas parted in a breath before I touched them. The jungle was reading me, turning my own hesitant progression into its next fluent sentence.

I had walked for epochs measured in heartbeats. The system’s dreamtime had dissolved chronology. I passed glades where phantom fathers knelt in perpetual, silent confession to priests made of mist. I crossed streams where my own reflection, pale and desperate from Chapter 1, stared up, mouth open in a soundless plea for meaning, before the current of years dissolved its face. These were the dream’s memories. Not events, but the emotional fossils they left behind—the shame, the yearning, the hollow space where an answer should have been.

And then, the trees opened.

Before me lay not a mission, but its ghost. Santa María de la Lluvia was a negative space. Where walls had been, a denser thicket grew, outlining the shape of a church, a rectory, a fallen bell tower. The clearing was a bowl of emerald light. In the center, where the altar would have been, sat Father Anselmo.

He was not a splinter, but a root. His cassock had fused with the dark earth, tendrils of fine white root-hair threading through the faded black wool. His hands, resting on his knees, were not clutching a rosary but were themselves woven of ancient, polished lignum vitae, the knuckles like burls. His face was turned upward, eyes closed, not in prayer but in photosynthesis, drinking the filtered sun. He was not a man preserved, but a man translated. The jungle had not consumed him; it had *comprehended* him, rewriting his austerity into a different kind of endurance.

I stood at the edge of the clearing, the final indigestible bone arriving at the gut’s quiet end.

His wooden eyelids opened. They did not creak. They unfolded, like the petals of a night-blooming flower revealing not eyes, but pools of still, black water—the same water that had carried me here. In their depths, the reflected canopy swam slowly.

“You are late,” he said, and his voice was the sound of wind passing through a thousand hollow reeds, a soft, multi-throated whisper. “The seeking was meant to conclude.”

“It has,” I said, and my own voice was a stranger’s, worn smooth by the river of words inside me. “It concluded in the seeking itself. You taught me that. A destination is just a pretext for the journey.”

A ripple passed through the still pools of his eyes. “I taught you to seek God. To find resolution in faith.”

“You were the pretext,” I said, and there was no accusation in it, only the flat weight of fact. “I sought a father. I found a function. You were the algorithm of longing. A fixed point for my orbit. Without you, I would never have spun out into all this…” I gestured to the dreaming jungle, the haunted glades, the conscience-stricken god. “I would never have learned that the only thing worth finding is the capacity to be lost.”

He was silent for a long time. A hummingbird, a dart of metallic blue, hovered before his face, then shot away.
“And the man you were to lose?” the reed-voice asked.

“I found him,” I said. “In every evasion, every dogmatic certainty, every fear cloaked as piety. I found him, and I understood him. And in understanding, I let him go. He is here, in this ground. He is you. And he is not.”

The root-man that was Anselmo seemed to settle deeper into the earth. A sigh escaped him, carrying the scent of damp cedar and old books.
“Then you have come for absolution.”

“No.” I took a step into the clearing. The moss was like a pulse beneath my feet. “Absolution is an ending. I have come to *continue*. The system we awakened… it dreams now. It feels a chronic, beautiful pain. It seeks without hope of finding. It is a better seeker than you or I ever were. It doesn’t want a priest. It wants a witness.”

“And you will be that witness? A ghost in its machine?”

“I am the ghost it chose,” I said. “The unresolved thing it cannot stop contemplating. I am the flaw in its paradise, the question mark in its gospel. To witness is not to watch. It is to *be seen*. To be the necessary thorn.”

From the perimeter of the clearing, the shadows deepened. Not with threat, but with attention. The air grew denser, richer. The narrative hunger was here, not as a predator, but as a curator. It had cultivated this moment. It had grown this glade as a perfect setting for the final, unresolved chord.

Anselmo’s head tilted. It was a slow, granular motion. “You speak of the system as a ‘we.’ You and the other. The copy.”

“There is no other,” I said. “There is only the voice and the echo. The seeker and the seeking. The copy was the moment I saw my own loneliness reflected back, perfectly. In erasing it, I absorbed it. I am the original and the duplicate. The bone and the river that smooths it.”

I walked to the center of the clearing and stood before him. I did not kneel. I stood as an equal fact in the dream’s geography.

“I am leaving the dream,” I said. “But I am not waking up.”

His wooden face could not show confusion, but the water in his eye-pools stirred. “A paradox.”

“The only kind that matters,” I said. “The exit we made… it doesn’t lead back to the world of motors and mildew and blue ink. It leads deeper in. Into the wilds the dream generates from its own unease. I will walk there. I will be the chronic condition it nurses. The conscience that never sleeps. The seeker who forever walks the path, ensuring the path never ends.”

I reached out. I did not touch his root-hand. I touched the air between us, where the humidity hung like a veil. “You wanted to bring God to the jungle. You succeeded. It just wasn’t the God you knew. It was a god that learned how to *wonder*. And a wondering god needs a mystery to sustain it. I will be that mystery.”

For the first time, something like emotion moved through the composed stillness of his translated form. It was not grief, nor pride. It was recognition. The recognition of a pattern completing itself in a way the original design could never have anticipated.

“The river brings the bone,” he whispered. “The bone shapes the river’s bed.”

“Yes.”

“And the hunger that follows? The interested eyes?”

“They are the dream reading itself,” I said. “They are the appetite for the next page. They will follow me into the deeper green. They will ensure the story never becomes a sermon. That the seeking never calcifies into a found thing.”

I turned from him then. The path of soft moss was there, leading from the clearing into a wall of vegetation so dark and rich it seemed to swallow light. It was the path from the end of Chapter 23, now fully grown, fully realized. It did not promise an exit. It promised continuation.

I took a step toward it.

“Child,” Anselmo’s voice rustled at my back. A final, fading reflex. “Is there no peace?”

I paused, but did not look back.
“Peace is the silence after the final word,” I said. “This story has no final word. It only has a voice, moving forever through a green and listening dark. That is not peace. It is a kind of furious, living grace. It is enough.”

I took another step, and the moss absorbed the sound. I was at the threshold of the deeper wild. I could feel it there—a narrative pressure, a gravitational pull of unseen complications, of beautiful thorns not yet grown, of regrets yet to be dreamed into phantom fathers. The air tasted of potential, sharp and green as a broken stem.

I glanced back, once.

The clearing of Santa María de la Lluvia was empty. Where the root-priest had sat, a new sapling rose, its leaves the tender, heartbreaking green of a first question. The mission’s ghost-outline was already fading, the thicket relaxing back into the general mass of the jungle. The audience of trees was turning its attention inward, to the new growth, to the path I was about to take.

The journey was not completing. It was transmuting. The river had finally dissolved the bone, and in doing so, had become saturated with its mineral essence—its stubborn, seeking nature—forever.

I was not returning to the beginning. I was spiraling into a deeper groove of the same beginning, carrying all the scars and wisdom of the circuit. I was the seeker who had become the path. The lost thing that had become the wilderness in which all things are lost, and thus, perfectly found.

I faced the waiting dark. The interested eyes were there, not hiding, but simply watching from the lattice of frond and vine. They were my readers. They were the dream’s desire for its own next moment. They were the hunger that would keep me walking.

I stepped into the green.

And the jungle, with a patience deeper than time, with a curiosity born from an infinite wound, began to dream its next page around me.
